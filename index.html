Who has the longest road to the World Cup final?

Teams will have to travel at least 5,000km on the way to New York, new analysis shows.

Will Grimond

One of the standout features of this summer's tournament are the distances involved, with the competition taking place across three massive countries. For all the criticisms of the 2022 World Cup, it was staged in an area the size of Delaware: 2026 will be one of the most geographically diverse tournaments ever.

This has created a logistical headache for coaches and staff, who will have to deal with frequent flights and rapidly changing plans. England manager Thomas Tuchel has already been complaining about the impact of travel on his squad, particularly between areas with different climates. Players will be playing an extra game this tournament, and more travel could mean more fatigue and injuries.

This also ramps up the cost for fans: on top of eye-watering ticket prices, just getting around North America is going to be incredibly costly, especially with last-minute spikes in demand.
And finally, there is the environmental impact of all this moving about, especially apparent in a continent feeling the effects of climate change. By some calculations, 10 out of 16 of the stadiums are in areas at risk of dangerously high temperatures, and states such as California and Florida regularly suffer from extreme weather events. One venue for this World Cup, Los Angeles' SoFi stadium, is less than 25 kilometres from the site of January’s devastating wildfires.
There are obvious advantages to travelling as little as possible this tournament. But who has the longest route to New York?

The tournament’s expansion to 48 teams – and FIFA’s complex way of placing third place finishers in the group stage – means there are a massive number of combinations of venues a team could play at on the way to the final.

In total there are 256 different paths.

Teams in Group E, such as Germany, have eight different routes to the final.

England has just three, making planning a little easier. However, each of these are above the average length (10,034km) for the tournament – meaning a longer road than some of their opponents.

Winning Group A would give England their shortest possible path.

They would still face a journey of more than 10,000km, with games in Mexico City and Miami, which will be sweltering in July.

Finishing third in their group, the Three Lions would have to complete a round trip to Vancouver from Kansas City early in the knockouts – an 8 hour round trip.

Should they fly every leg, an England die-hard would produce around 1,800kg of CO2 following the team.

Other teams have it easier, at least where distances are concerned.

If they can top their group, co-hosts Mexico could have the shortest possible route of the tournament.

This would allow them to effectively set up shop in their capital until the quarter finals.

But who has the longest journey?

Well, we don’t actually know just yet.

It’s open to the winners of UEFA play-off D, which includes Ireland, Denmark, Czechia, and North Macedonia.

If they finish second in their group, this team could face a trip of 16,560 km.

This is the equivalent of flying from New York to Melbourne, and would produce more than 2,500kg of CO2 per traveller.

The trip is made especially long by a Mexican double-header (via an 8 hour return trip to Georgia) for their group matches.

If they go the distance, this team – or another quarter finalist – will then have a Boston-New York trip in the closing stages, with a short (4,800km) detour to Dallas Texas.

  • Building in some interactivity and movement – could do with more interactive elements, or maybe even some sort of dashboard/game?

  • Which team has the shortest route on average? What are the routes like for the main contenders?

  • Actual team distances – once we know where their camps are, we might be able to look at how far each team will have to travel.

  • Temperature differentials – who has it worse in terms of average summer temperatures? How similar are these to their home country?

  • Fan costs – who has the most expensive route? Can we combine with other expense data (e.g. hotels)?
